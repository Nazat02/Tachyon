Why a tachyon, why now

Special relativity doesn't forbid faster-than-light particles — it just makes their mass imaginary and erases their rest frame. Tachyon turns that one algebraic flip into four explorable 3D shapes, each one a different consequence of the same broken sign.

Four boards, one particle

Each mode is its own small lecture — drag the slider, rotate the camera, and watch the consequence play out in three dimensions.

01

Energy–momentum E² = p²c² − m²c⁴

Flip the sign on m² and the two-sheeted hyperboloid of an ordinary particle opens into one continuous surface with a throat at |p| = √(−m²). There's no point on it where the particle is at rest.

02

Light cone Δx > cΔt

A tachyon's worldline runs outside the light cone, linking two events no slower-than-light signal could connect. Different observers disagree about which one happened first — the seed of the tachyonic-antitelephone paradox.

03

Imaginary mass m = iμ, μ ∈ ℝ

Writing m² = −μ² makes the rest mass itself imaginary. Below the threshold |p| = μ, the real energy–momentum relation has no real solution — the surface dips into a region with no physical particle on it.

04

Cherenkov shock cosθ_c = c / v

A charged tachyon can't avoid this one: it sheds vacuum Cherenkov photons into a forward shock cone. Losing energy this way makes it speed up further — a runaway with no terminal velocity, the mirror image of ordinary Cherenkov radiation.

Office hours

The questions that come up after the lecture, answered the way they'd get answered after class.

Q.Do tachyons actually exist?
As far as every experiment to date can tell, no. They're a legal solution to the relativistic energy–momentum equation, not a confirmed particle — closer to a "what would have to be true" than a "here's one in a detector." Tachyon treats that distinction honestly: it's visualizing the math, not claiming a discovery.
Q.Then why does any of this matter?
Because the consequences of m² < 0 are fully determined the moment you write the equation down — causality violations, imaginary energy thresholds, a Cherenkov cone with no terminal speed. Working through what a forbidden object would look like is a standard way physicists stress-test what "forbidden" really means.
Q.Why does the cone glow at the throat?
That's the locus where |p| = √(−m²) — the minimum momentum a real solution can have. It's not decorative; the brightness is standing in for how close a state sits to that boundary, the same way a professor might circle the one point on a diagram that actually matters.
Q.Can I use this in an actual class?
Yes — open any of the four lectures, drag the slider in front of students, and let them watch the surface deform instead of describing it verbally. No login, no install, just the board.
